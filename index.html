Research Challenge

The new app represented significant investment in user experience improvements based on North American research‚Äîstreamlined navigation, integrated digital wallet payments, and refined checkout flows. Before rolling out globally, leadership needed to understand how these North America-optimized patterns would perform in APAC markets where user behaviors, payment ecosystems, and digital commerce maturity levels differed significantly.

Our target region: Taiwan, Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines‚Äîmarkets representing a substantial concentration of Herbalife's active buyer base and diverse cultural contexts that could reveal critical localization needs.

Language Assumption Testing

Why Multi-Method Research?

Testing the language assumption required going beyond traditional usability studies. While think-aloud sessions could reveal comprehension issues with copy, they wouldn't expose deeper problems with cultural relevance of product organization and information structure.

Our approach: We combined usability testing (to observe language comprehension and task completion) with tree testing (to reveal whether product taxonomy and mental models aligned across cultures). This dual approach allowed us to distinguish between surface-level translation issues and fundamental structural mismatches.

The goal wasn't just findings‚Äîit was capability building: We needed to answer critical questions with enough rigor that design could move forward confidently, while also creating clear opportunities for both visual design changes and information architecture improvements. With robust baseline data established, junior researchers could take on lower-risk follow-up studies like A/B testing design iterations and copy variations with mentoring support from me as lead researcher.

Key Questions Driving the Research

Our multi-method approach was designed to answer several interconnected questions about cross-cultural product experience

Comprehension vs. Cultural Fit
Can APAC users understand the English copy (comprehension), and does that copy feel appropriate and trustworthy in their cultural context (cultural fit)? These are distinct questions requiring different research methods.
Checkout Flow Pain Points
Where do users experience friction in the purchase journey? How do payment method expectations differ? What information do users need at each stage to feel confident proceeding?
Information Architecture Alignment
Do APAC users organize products and categories the same way North American users do? Does the navigation structure match their mental models and browsing behaviors?
Trust Signal Priorities
What information helps APAC users make confident purchase decisions? Do they prioritize the same content (reviews, certifications, ingredients) as North American users?
Design Iteration Prioritization
Which changes would have the highest impact on conversion? How should we sequence visual design updates versus information architecture changes?
Research Capability Building
What baseline data and frameworks could enable junior researchers to conduct follow-up studies independently? Where could low-risk A/B testing accelerate iteration?

Key Findings & Recommendations

Our research revealed five critical differences between APAC and US buying behaviors, each translating directly into actionable design recommendations.

Social Proof Trumps Expert Authority
APAC buyers prioritized customer reviews and testimonials significantly more than expert endorsements.
Recommendation Redesign PDPs to feature reviews above-the-fold
Payment Method Expectations Create Barriers
68% of participants expected region-specific mobile wallet payments that weren't available in the current implementation.
Recommendation Integrate mobile wallets: GoPay, OVO, GCash, LINE Pay
Checkout Flow Expectations Diverge
76% of APAC participants repeatedly verified selections, preferring staged checkout over streamlined one-page flows.
Recommendation Multi-step checkout with clear progress indicators
Language Localization Beyond Translation
89% confused by imperial measurements; copy felt "too American" despite English proficiency.
Recommendation Cultural copy adaptation with local copywriters
Bundle & Subscription Mental Models Differ
78% of APAC users organized products around goal-based bundles versus individual items.
Recommendation Restructure taxonomy around wellness programs

Carrying Recommendations Through: Payment Method Integration

Following the research, I worked directly with the Checkout Team to identify key payment methods used in the APAC region by country, providing detailed insight into which systems should be prioritized for integration. This involved bench research in conjunction with fintech market forecasting and cross-referencing user responses from our study on which financial payment systems participants felt most comfortable with and used most frequently. This layered approach ensured our recommendations were grounded in both user behavior data and market viability.

Addressing the Key Assumption: English Language Proficiency

The finding that challenged the "English proficiency = comprehension" assumption was especially critical to socialize across stakeholder teams. While APAC users could technically understand the English copy, the language didn't feel appropriate, trustworthy, or culturally relevant‚Äîdirectly impacting purchase confidence and conversion.

Socializing the insight: I shared user interview clips and key quotes that powerfully demonstrated the gap between comprehension and cultural fit. Hearing participants say things like "The translation is correct, but it doesn't feel like it's written for me" was more persuasive than data alone in helping stakeholders understand the nuance.

Validating with A/B testing: To further strengthen the case, we conducted preliminary A/B studies testing simple word changes in CTAs and product descriptions. Results showed measurable improvements in key KPIs‚Äînotably increased user sentiment scores and higher rates of users moving products into cart. In this case, A/B testing was invaluable not just for optimization but for validation: it demonstrated how small, culturally-appropriate copy changes could quickly improve metrics that mattered to design teams, making the business case for broader localization efforts undeniable.
